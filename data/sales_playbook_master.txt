## OBJECTION 1: "Your price is too high."
Strategy: Value-Based Reframe (Never discount without anchoring value first)

Step 1 — Acknowledge without capitulating:
"I completely understand — price is always an important factor. Can I ask,
what are you currently paying for [their current solution]?"

Step 2 — Calculate the Cost of Inaction:
"Most of our clients were paying $X across 2-3 tools. We consolidated that
into one platform and they actually saved money. Let's do a quick ROI
calculation together — is that okay?"

Step 3 — Anchor to Outcomes:
"Based on your team size and current close rate, a 5% improvement in win
rate would generate approximately $Y in new revenue per year. Our cost is
$Z. That's a [X:1] ROI in year one alone."

Step 4 — Offer Value Before Price Concessions:
Before reducing price, try offering:
- Annual billing (saves 15%)
- Phased rollout starting with a pilot team of 10 reps
- Adding a bonus training workshop ($2,500 value, at no cost)
- Extended onboarding support for the first 90 days

NEVER lead with a discount. Always explore value adjustments first.

SECTION 4: NEGOTIATION TACTICS
================================================================================

## The "Never Give Without a Get" Principle
Every concession you make must be paired with something in return.

Examples:
- "I can reduce the price by 10%, but I'd need the contract signed by
  end of quarter and an annual commitment upfront."
- "I can add free onboarding if you agree to be a reference customer."
- "We can extend the pilot by 2 weeks if you commit to a decision date."

Giving without getting trains buyers to expect more concessions.

---

## Anchoring High
Always present full-list pricing first. Let the anchor set expectations.
Then negotiate from a position of strength.

---

## The "Assumptive Close"
At the right moment, move the conversation as if they've decided:
"So assuming this works for your team in the pilot, when would you
want to roll it out company-wide? Q2 or Q3?"

---

## Creating Urgency
Authentic urgency levers (NEVER fabricate these):
- End-of-quarter pricing lock: "Our pricing increases 12% on April 1st.
  If we sign today, I can lock in current rates."
- Implementation slots: "Our onboarding team only has 3 implementation
  slots left before June — would you like to reserve yours?"
- Promotional bundles: "This quarter we're offering the Revenue Intelligence
  Module free for the first year — that's a $20/user/month value."

---

## Multi-Threading
In enterprise deals, never have a single champion. Always build
relationships with:
1. The Economic Buyer (controls budget)
2. The Technical Buyer (evaluates fit)
3. The End User / Champion (uses the product daily)
4. The Legal / Procurement contact

If you lose your champion, the deal dies with them. Map the org early.

SECTION 9: QUALIFICATION FRAMEWORK (MEDDIC)
================================================================================

Always qualify deals using MEDDIC before moving to demo stage:

M — METRICS: What business outcome are they trying to achieve?
     "What does success look like in 6 months? How do you measure it?"

E — ECONOMIC BUYER: Who controls the budget?
     "Who is ultimately responsible for this decision?"

D — DECISION CRITERIA: How will they choose?
     "What are the top 3 factors you'll use to evaluate solutions?"

D — DECISION PROCESS: What are the steps to get to a signed contract?
     "What does your evaluation and procurement process look like?"

I — IDENTIFY PAIN: What is the cost of the status quo?
     "What's the biggest challenge your team is facing right now?"

C — CHAMPION: Who is your internal advocate?
     "Is there someone internally who will sponsor this project?"

Deals missing M, E, or C should be flagged as at-risk in the CRM.
